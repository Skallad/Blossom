Welcome to Blossom!

The symbolism of different flowers have been around for thousands of years. The birth flower on the other hand, though connected to flower symbolism and meaning can be gifted to someone, thought to also represent the person's characteristics, much like zodiac signs.

Birth flowers are believed to have been around since Roman times, and tradition in different cultures have carried the practice on through history to this day.

This site offers you to find out what your birth flower is. Head on over to the birth flower tab and click on the month to explore further the meaning of each birth flower!

January

Carnation & Snowdrop

Carnations often symbolise love and rememberance if the colours are in varying shades of red or pink, whereas purple carnations mean capriciousness and yellow symbolise disappointment and rejection. Striped carnations signify regret. carnations are often used during Mother's Day or Valentine's Day because of their meanings.

Snowdrops can be used on both happy and sad occasions. For celebrations, they symbolise optimism and hope and during occasions such as death or misfortune, they have compassion as their main symbolism. The snowdrop is strongly associated with innocence thanks to its white colour. Due to it often being the first flower to show up during early spring, it also can signify new beginnings and beauty!

February

Violet & Primrose

Violets are often associated with loyalty. Mainly, violets symbolise modesty, faithfulness, neverending love and rememberance. Violets also containt salicylic acid, which is a key ingredient in aspirin and has therefore been used as pain relievers when modern medicine wasn't where it is today. Nature has it all!

Primrose symbolises young love, youth and everlasting existence! The word "primrose" originates from Latin's 'primus', which means 'first' or 'early'. Primroses are often one of the first flowers in spring to bloom.

March

Daffodil & Jonquil

When winter turns to spring, nature stays strong and resilient and daffodils and jonquils do the same, therefore they have the same symbolism. These flowers look very similar but can be told apart by their leaves. The daffodil blooms into a bright yellow only, while the jonquil can be found in different varieties, like white, peach or pink.

April

Daisy & Sweet Pea

Daisies are humble, symbols of purity, loyalty and innocence but also believed to symbolise motherhood. It is often gifted to new mothers.

According to the language of flowers, sweet peas represent goodbyes and thank you for the lovely time. It is a great gift for someone that is going away somewhere. Bouquets of sweet peas represent blissful pleasure.

May

Lily of the Valley & Hawthorn

If you are like the creator of this website, born in the month of May, that is, you get to claim the same birth flowers as her! The lily of the valley has symbolic meaning in purity, humility and sweetness. It also only blooms during a short window of time during spring, meaning that they are not seen in the wilderness that often.

Hawthorn or sometimes the May tree, a type of shrub, known for its berry-like fruits, blooms in May. The flowers that bloom are symbols of protection and love for the things around you, but also thanks to the hawthorn's fruits, they also symbolise fertility.

June

Rose & Honeysuckle

The very popular and widespread Rose have long been around for their symbol of love and passion. Red roses indicate romantic love and yellow ones friendship.

Honeysuckle flowers symbolise pure happiness, affection and sweetness but also devotion and everlasting bonds.

July

Larkspur & Water Lily

Larkspur symbolises dignity and positivity adn the maening for the flower can vary depending on the colour. The flower in itself symbolises a heart, open, loving and welcoming.

Water lilies signify innocence, fertility and purity.

August

Gladiolus & Poppy

The Gladiolus symbolises strength, integrity in morals and generosity.

The Poppy, with different meanings all over the world can symbolise things such as relaxation and recovery. After WWI, the flower has come to symbolise a hope for a peaceful future.

September

Aster & Morning Glory

Asters are flowers of love, wisdom, valour and faith. The name for the flower comes from the Greek word for star, probably because of its shape, similar to that of a twinkling star.

The morning glory only last one day and has because of that carried with it a symbol of love, the essence of life, affection as well as spontaneity.

October

Marigold & Cosmos

The marigold, known for its resilience and being hardy duting autumn, symbolises a strong will and determination to succeed.

The cosmos signify peace and tranquility. Deep emotions like sincerity, everlasting love and loyalty are also associated with this flower.

November

Chrysanthemum

The single birth flower, symbolising well wishes, joy, and bringing good luck can be used for many occasions thanks to it being available in a wide range of colours, which in turn have specific meanings themselves.

December

Narcissus & Holly

We have reached the end of the year, but don't fret for the narcissus is here to cheer you up! The narcissus has a cheerful yellow colour and symbolises new beginnings, rebirth and rejuvenation. Thanks to its ability to bloom year after year they also represent faithfulness.

Of course, 'tis the season! Who could go a Christmas without thinking of the holly? Their flowers symbolise life itself thanks to their evergreen leaves. If the Holly is in a wreath, they represent friendship and faithful love.
